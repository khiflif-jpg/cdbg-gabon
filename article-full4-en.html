Gabon’s wood economy: from harvest to finished products in 2025

The wood sector remains a cornerstone of Gabon’s diversification strategy. In 2023 it accounted for about 3.2% of GDP and 6% of exports, while representing the country’s largest source of formal private employment, according to the World Bank. Budget revenues linked to forestry reached roughly CFA 41.9 billion in 2023 (about 0.3% of GDP), almost quadrupling since 2016. These indicators confirm the relevance of the shift toward local processing initiated after the log-export ban and consolidated by industrial clusters.

Resource and forest management: a strategic natural asset

Gabon is among the most forested countries worldwide, with a predominant forest cover. Industrial operations are framed by approved management plans, systematic inventories, and reduced-impact logging methods. Certification is progressing and strengthens credibility on origin-sensitive markets. This combination of management, control and certification underpins value creation while preserving the natural capital.

Local processing: from sawmills to plywood and finished goods

The ban on log exports accelerated investment in sawmilling, peeling and plywood manufacturing, with okoumé at the core of product lines. Integrated plants convert roundwood into technical veneers, panels, and interior components and furniture. Upgrading is visible in machining, finishing and design, as project-oriented workshops target export standards. Over the medium term, value will be driven by better recovery rates, consistent quality and product certification.

Nkok SEZ: an accelerator for industrialization and exports

The Nkok Special Economic Zone brings together processors, control services, logistics and administrative facilitators in one ecosystem. This concentration reduces operational frictions, improves traceability and streamlines shipments. Veneer/plywood value chains are particularly dynamic. For SMEs, the SEZ acts as a gateway to demanding markets by offering a shared framework for compliance and specialized services.

2024–2025 market context: late-2024 slowdown, 2025 adjustments

Late 2024 saw a contraction in sector activity: the index declined by 6.6% in Q4-2024 (Direction Générale de l’Économie via ATIBT). Contributing factors included softer external demand on some segments and logistical and energy frictions. In 2025, international demand for okoumé panels remains mixed across regions; differentiation through compliance (legality, traceability, certification) and technical quality remains decisive to secure better valuation in European and North American markets.

International compliance: EUDR, CITES and certification

The EU’s new regulation on deforestation-free products (EUDR) is a game changer. Unless further regulatory changes occur, application is scheduled for 30 December 2025 for large and medium-sized operators and 30 June 2026 for micro and small companies. Market access to the EU will require geolocation of origin plots and documented due diligence for each shipment. For species listed under CITES, permits and controls are reinforced, with strict traceability to protect the integrity of export flows. Scaling up forest management certifications (e.g., FSC/PEFC) supports alignment and lowers perceived risk among buyers.

Competitiveness: energy, logistics and skills

Energy costs and inland logistics remain areas of attention for industry. Responses combine network reliability, energy recovery from by-products (biomass), maintenance of road and port corridors, and continuous improvement in mill recovery. Skills needs concentrate on industrial maintenance, quality, compliance documentation and product design. Over time, vertical integration (peeling → panels → finished goods) and quality standardization strengthen productivity and export value.

Outlook 2025–2027: premium on traceability and quality

The 2025–2027 trajectory will hinge on the collective ability to deliver robust geolocated traceability, generalize certification and maintain competitive lead times and finishes. Under this scenario, access to premium markets (EU/North America) consolidates, while Asian markets remain decisive in volume. With these levers, Gabon can position itself as an African hub for responsible wood transformation.
